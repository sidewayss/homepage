What is In One eaR → Out the OtheR?

It is an album of songs, in a format that merges these two dictionary definitions of "album":

• A scrapbook, a binder for storing and organizing keepsakes and mementos

• A collection of audio recordings packaged together on a disk, download, or other digital storage medium

Like a scrapbook, the empty "pages" in this album will be filled in gradually over the course of (hopefully) several years. The current schedule amounts to a song a month, though I'll be pairing most of the songs as A/B sides, and releasing two songs every two months. At the time of the initial release, I have eleven songs that I am committed to releasing. Over the course of the next eleven months I hope to gain enough traction (and money) to continue beyond that.

It's a public scrapbook, and I am taking an extra step in that direction by publishing all the individual audio tracks and DAW sessions for anyone to download, remix, and republish. I want to encourage people to screw around with my material. I may not personally enjoy every remix or cover version of my songs, but who cares about my opinion? I don't, and you certainly shouldn't. I want to encourage people to experiment freely and unabashedly. The material lends itself to a certain lack of restraint, and I want people to have fun with it.

In my mind, this all the remixes and other remakes of my material are part of the album. My current situation makes hosting all of that implausible, so I am currently relying on external platforms for all of that infrastructure. You can download tracks at blend.io, here. What's the deal with censorship?

I understand that many people don't enjoy foul language, my mother included. I also respect the desires of parents to censor media for their children. So in spite of the fact that I enjoy an overtly nasty turn of phrase, I sympathize with those who don't, and I want them to be able to enjoy this album too.

Rewriting the lyrics is much better than bleeping them out. In keeping with the superficial nature of the album, I focus more on the rhymes and general tone, rather than matching semantic meaning across the censored and uncensored versions. That can make for some entertaining and sometimes surprising semantics.

Add that all up and you get Sideways Skullfinger. It's a lot of extra work for me, but I think it's worth it.
